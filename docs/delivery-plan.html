1What we are doing

Midnight Passport gives a user a named, self-sovereign, privacy-preserving identity on the Midnight network — without ever seeing a seed phrase, a raw address, or a gas-token purchase screen. The project's north star is the secure-onboarding-design authored by Charles Hoskinson, which sets out six design principles for how identity, custody, and signing should behave in the ecosystem.

The six principles, quoted briefly from the source:

  1. Keys never leave TEEs; authorisation via ZK witnesses.
  2. Single seed, three wallets (Shielded, Night, Dust).
  3. Chain abstraction via intent model.
  4. One key per device.
  5. Privacy-by-design identity.
  6. Seedless UX.

Delivering all six principles in their final form in one go is not realistic; several are open research areas in their own right. The design also has implications well beyond the ARC boundary. We have therefore been engaging with the wider ecosystem from the start.

Who we have been talking with

  • Midnight Foundation — aligning on the platform surface (curves, proof pipeline, APIs, CAIP namespace), the Midnight Improvement Proposal (MIP) process, and ensuring the architecture we build is one the Foundation recognises as the Midnight-native direction.
  • Lace — the team building Lace ID. Their existing key-derivation and address implementation directly informs the first two standards we plan to publish: the key-derivation MIP and the Bech32 address-format MIP. Their ID product is also a natural consumer of the credential layer we will add later.
  • Cryptographic reviewer (Jesus Diaz Vico) and formal methods (James Chapman's team) — per-phase review and long-running rigour, respectively.

How we plan to get there

We cannot build everything at once. The plan is:

  1. Pick a subset of the principles to deliver fully in a first MVP.
  2. Accept honest, explicit compromises on the rest — not drop them, defer them to a named later step.
  3. Start building immediately, with a weekly demo as the unit of progress. Every Friday, one artefact that has moved. Over a few months, enough artefacts accumulate that they compose into the MVP.
  4. Use each weekly update both for credibility and for concrete evidence — we show a working thing, we say what did not work, we commit to next week's artefact.

Parts of the plan are already de-risked. Internal experiments have validated that Schnorr signatures verify inside a Compact circuit on a local Midnight devnet, and that Rust and TypeScript signing sides produce identical outputs across the language boundary. We have also located an audited, MIT-licensed FROST-on-JubJub implementation that already exists as an open-source Rust crate — the threshold-signatures crate published by the NEAR-MPC project. We consume that crate as a library dependency; this is not an integration with the NEAR blockchain or protocol. Other decisions (crate-consumption strategy, recovery mechanism, wallet partner) remain open but have a default direction and a decide-by date.

Why this matters beyond Passport

The ultimate goal is not an isolated wallet. It is a set of protocols and standards — published as MIPs — that the rest of the Midnight ecosystem can build on. With these in place, any wallet, any dApp, any credential issuer can plug into the same names, addresses, signatures, connection protocol, and credential system, without inventing its own. That interoperability is what turns Midnight Passport from an IOG product into a piece of ecosystem infrastructure — and what turns a user's privacy-preserving, self-custodial, multi-device experience from a bespoke app feature into a property of the network itself. User-friendly by default.

The north star, in one sentence

Define the identity, custody, and connection primitives of Midnight so that a user scans a QR code, lands on a named account, and moves across the network and its dApps in a way that is privacy-preserving, self-custodial, interoperable — and effortless.

2Delivery horizon

Getting from today to the end-state takes roughly a year and change. The scope of that end-state is set by the design; the sequence is set by one rule: deliver user-facing value in the first MVP, then strengthen the architecture underneath without changing the user experience.

Each step below is an independently demoable product. The user experience is stable across steps — what improves is the trust model underneath. We target roughly a 90-day beat between major steps after the MVP.

Why UX first

A plan that holds the user experience constant across every step is the plan that can keep stakeholders aligned for a year. At each step, the user sees the same product getting better at what it already does — while we progressively remove scaffolding (centralised signing, account-provider-held device mapping) and replace it with the decentralised machinery the design principles require. No step forces a re-onboarding. No step feels like a reset.

2.1MVP 1 — end of June 2026

MVP 1 Apr → end of June 2026 · 10 weeks to demo, Friday 26 June
The smallest product that delivers on the spirit of the design

Principles we deliver

  • Principle 6 · Seedless UX — DELIVERED. Passkey (WebAuthn) authentication from day one. The user never sees a seed, a mnemonic, or a raw address.
  • Principle 1 · Authorisation via ZK witnesses — PARTIAL. The ZK-witness authorisation model is fully in place. Custody of the key sits in a single managed signing service rather than on the device — we choose the correct pattern now and move custody later.
  • Principle 2 · Single seed, three wallets — PARTIAL. Three wallets derived from one seed via CIP-1852 hierarchical derivation. The seed lives in the signing service; it moves onto the device in v2.0 Stream B.
  • Principle 3 · Chain abstraction — ACHIEVABLE. The adopted threshold-signatures crate already supports ECDSA, EdDSA, and FROST, so chain abstraction becomes an API layer on top, not a new crypto build. We do not expose cross-chain flows in MVP 1 but the substrate for them exists.

Principles we defer

  • Principle 4 · One key per device — deferred to MVP 1.1. The MVP has one key in the signing service and multiple devices authenticating via passkey. Same UX (multi-device), different mechanism.
  • Principle 5 · Privacy-by-design identity (credentials) — deferred to v3.0. Not blocked by the MVP — it is independent of the custody model, so it can land later without architectural rework.

Compromises we accept (and name)

  • Single signing node rather than the federated n-of-t network. Acknowledged trust model for the MVP; federation lands in MVP 1.1.
  • No on-chain name registry — alice.midnight names are held off-chain in the account provider for the MVP. Registry lands in MVP 1.1.
  • No QR onboarding — web-passkey only. QR returns in MVP 1.1 with mitigations for channel-binding and swap attacks.

What ships at the June demo

Passkey login → named account → wallet connects (Lace or stub) → capability grant approved in the management console → transaction signed → submitted to Midnight devnet. Plus a recovery sad-path. Plus a "what's next" slide pointing to MVP 1.1.

2.2MVP 1.1 — multi-device accounts

MVP 1.1 ~90-day effort cycle · begins after MVP 1 ships
Names, federation, and the connection protocol as a MIP

MVP 1.1 is the first post-MVP beat. It adds the features we cut from MVP 1 and hardens the MVP scaffolding. Concretely:

  • Multi-device accounts (from the user's perspective) — a single account (alice.midnight) authenticated by multiple passkeys, including adding a new device. The device-to-account mapping is still held by the account provider at this step; it moves on-chain in v2.0 Stream A.
  • Federated signing — replaces the single MVP signing node with an n-of-t federation (default n=5, t=4), using the same adopted Rust crate.
  • Name registry — alice.midnight names become on-chain, with commit-reveal registration, multi-address resolution, and anti-squat mitigations.
  • QR onboarding returns with the channel-binding, timestamp, and visual-confirmation mitigations we've already specified.
  • The dApp-wallet connection protocol — our top-priority ecosystem MIP — becomes a full MIP with named external co-authors. This is the formal interface every wallet and dApp in the Midnight ecosystem will build against.

Principles landed at MVP 1.1: none new (principles are fully landed in later steps) but principle 1 strengthens from "single trusted operator" to "federation" and the user-facing feature set gains multi-device and named accounts.

2.3v2.0 Stream A — multi-key account contract

v2.0 · Stream A ~90-day effort cycle · runs in parallel with Stream B
Move the device-to-account mapping onto the chain

Stream A delivers principle 4 (one key per device) in its full form. The account becomes a smart-contract object on Midnight that represents a set of authorised device keys. Any registered device key can authorise transactions via a ZK witness. The account provider no longer holds the device-to-account mapping — the contract does.

The stream also introduces scoped function-call keys for dApp authorisation with bounded permissions (expiry, value cap, allowed contracts), which is what turns the dApp connection protocol from a session grant into a typed capability.

Stream A is independent of Stream B. It can ship while the signing network is still operating. MIP-4 lands alongside the implementation.

2.4v2.0 Stream B — on-device cryptography

v2.0 · Stream B ~90-day effort cycle · runs in parallel with Stream A
Keys and proofs on the user's phone

Stream B delivers principles 1 and 2 in full. The user's key moves into the device's trusted execution environment (TEE) — Secure Enclave on iOS, StrongBox / TEE on Android — with AES-256-GCM wrapping. Zero-knowledge proof generation runs on-device. A decentralised recovery protocol (DeRec or equivalent) replaces the signing network's implicit recovery model.

With Stream B complete, the signing network retires. The user now has full self-custody: keys on their own hardware, proofs generated locally, recovery decentralised. The user experience is unchanged.

Stream B is independent of Stream A. It can ship with a single-device model if Stream A has not landed; conversely, Stream A can run while Stream B is still in progress. Both must complete before v3.0.

2.5v3.0 and beyond — credentials, KYC, chain abstraction

v3.0+ successive 90-day effort cycles · after v2.0 completes · sub-tracks may parallelise
The additive layer built on a decentralised foundation

With self-custody delivered (v2.0 Stream B) and multi-device on-chain (v2.0 Stream A), v3.0 adds the features that need that foundation:

  • Privacy-preserving credentials MIP — principle 5 delivered. Selective disclosure of identity attributes via zero-knowledge. Built on Midnight's attestation-tree primitive. Multi-issuer from day one (zkMe and alternatives).
  • KYC and compliance — verifiable credentials issued by trusted third parties, reusable across dApps without re-verification.
  • Cross-chain intent engine — principle 3 delivered in full. Intent-based transactions that settle on Midnight, other chains, or both, from the same account.
  • Social account linking — a CEO-personal feature from the design, carried here because it naturally attaches to the credential layer.
  • DeRec social recovery — if not already delivered in Stream B, finalised here.

v3.0 is additive — it does not require changes to the custody architecture. It continues the 90-day cadence.

Principle-to-date summary

Principle 6 (seedless) lands at MVP 1. Principle 4 (one key per device) at v2.0 Stream A. Principles 1 and 2 (TEE keys; single seed) at v2.0 Stream B. Principles 3 and 5 (chain abstraction; privacy-preserving identity) at v3.0. Every principle has a date.

3The road to the MVP

Ten weeks from scope-lock to the MVP demo on Friday 26 June 2026, plus an effort-based post-MVP handover block that rolls straight into MVP 1.1. One Friday demo per week. Each week below states what we expect to see, what "success" means that week, and how slippage or success affects the weeks that follow.

How to read each week

  • Expect — the concrete thing we plan to show Friday. Not a status; a demo.
  • Success means — the specific criterion by which we will know the week worked.
  • Affects — how this week's outcome changes the downstream plan. Slipping Week n does not slip Week n+1 automatically; this line states what it actually affects.
  • Driving — the named person or pair leading the work.

Week-start dates are Mondays. The MVP demo target is Friday 26 June 2026 — Week 10. Weeks 11–12 of the classical 12-week window fall into July and are described as a single effort-based post-MVP block rather than calendar weeks.

Slippage protocol (applies to every week)

If the week's planned demo is not on track by Wednesday, the team must pick an adjacent deliverable (a smaller component, a predecessor step, or a parallel artefact from another lane) and show that Friday instead. Showing a working adjacent thing preserves the weekly rhythm; apologising for a missed week destroys it. Two consecutive slippages on the same critical-path item trigger an escalation at the next phase-boundary review.
